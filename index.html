01 — The Engine
The Wong-Wang model:
simulating a brain from wiring diagrams
Deco, Ponce-Alvarez, Mantini, Romani, Hagmann & Corbetta · J. Neuroscience, 2013

The central question this paper tackles is deceptively simple: if you know how brain regions are physically connected, can you predict how they'll talk to each other at rest? The answer, they show, is yes — but only when the model operates at a very specific dynamical "sweet spot".

The core idea in one sentence: Each brain region is a tiny noisy oscillator; regions connected by white-matter fibres push and pull on each other; when you tune the global coupling just right, the pattern of correlated activity that emerges matches what fMRI sees in resting humans.

The model borrows from earlier work by Wong & Wang (2006)A mathematical reduction that describes a population of NMDA-coupled neurons as a single equation. Instead of tracking millions of spikes, you track one number per region: the average amount of NMDA channels open (called S, the "synaptic gating variable").. Each brain region is reduced to one number — Si, how open its NMDA-receptor channels are — and all 66 regions are coupled together through the real structural connectomeA matrix where each entry tells you how many white-matter fibres run between two brain regions. Measured in living humans using diffusion MRI (tractography). Think of it as the brain's postal road network..

The key equation

CORE MODEL
dSi/dt = −Si/τ + γ·(1−Si)·H(xi) + σ·noise

// H(x) = transfer function: input current → firing rate
// x_i depends on: own recurrent activity + G × (SC × S)
// G = global coupling — the one parameter you fit

The magic is in G — a single number that scales how strongly regions influence each other. Too low: every region does its own thing, no structure. Too high: everything synchronises into one giant blob. The sweet spot — usually G ≈ 2 — is where the model's resting-state correlations best match real fMRI data. This is called the working point.

Why this matters for your project: Your code does exactly this G-sweep. The fit_G() function in g_fitting.py sweeps G from 0.5 to 4.0, runs simulations at each value, and picks the G that maximises how well simulated FC matches empirical HC FC. That's the entire Deco 2013 contribution in one function.

02 — The Heterogeneity Upgrade
Not all brain regions are equal:
heterogeneous local circuits matter
Demirtaş, Burt, Helmer et al. · Neuron, 2019

Deco 2013's model treated every brain region as identical — same local parameters everywhere, only the wiring differed. Demirtaş et al. showed this is wrong in an important way: the cortex is not uniform. Primary visual cortex and the prefrontal cortex aren't just differently connected — they're intrinsically different in how strongly their local circuits recur on themselves.

The key measurement they used is the T1w/T2w ratioAn MRI contrast ratio that reflects how much myelin (the fatty sheath around axons) is present in cortical grey matter. High T1w/T2w = heavily myelinated = lower sensory areas (visual, motor). Low T1w/T2w = lightly myelinated = higher association areas (prefrontal, default mode). It turns out this gradient perfectly predicts local circuit excitability. — a proxy for myelin content — which traces a gradient from early sensory areas (high myelination, strong local recurrence) to association areas like the prefrontal cortex (low myelination, weaker local recurrence).

The key finding: When they let each region's excitatory parameters (wEE, wEI) vary along this cortical hierarchy, the model's simulated FC matched real FC dramatically better than the homogeneous version. The sensory-association gradient in resting-state activity only emerges when local heterogeneity is included.

This paper also makes a crucial forward-looking point that is exactly what your project does:

Direct quote (paraphrased): "Heterogeneity maps could reflect varying distributions of receptor subtypes, neuronal cell types, and neuromodulators. These maps could be derived from gene expression, positron emission tomography, or autoradiography. Large-scale models can be fit to pharmacological neuroimaging, through simulation of hypothesised parameter perturbations that follow the expression topography of affected receptors."

That's Hansen 2022 + your PET maps + your perturbations. They literally described your project in their discussion section.

03 — The Atlas
A map of the brain's chemical landscape:
19 receptors, 1,200 people
Hansen, Shafiei, Markello et al. · Nature Neuroscience, 2022

If Demirtaş 2019 said "use receptor density maps to parameterise your model", Hansen 2022 is the paper that built those maps. It's a resource paper — its primary contribution is compiling and validating a comprehensive PET-derived atlas of receptor densities across the entire human cortex.

They collected PET scansPositron Emission Tomography. You inject a radioactive molecule that binds specifically to one type of receptor (e.g. NMDA receptors). Where the molecule accumulates tells you where those receptors are dense. Each scan costs around £3,000 and requires a cyclotron nearby. Hansen et al. pooled data from labs worldwide to avoid having to do all 19 scans themselves. from over 1,200 healthy individuals across multiple labs worldwide, covering 19 different receptors and transporters across 9 neurotransmitter systems. For each receptor, you get a map of the human cortex showing where that receptor is densest.

Key finding 1: Receptor density profiles are correlated with structural connectivity — regions that are anatomically connected tend to have similar receptor fingerprints. Chemistry and wiring co-evolve.
Key finding 2: Receptor distributions predict which disorders affect which brain regions. NMDA receptor density maps correlate with where schizophrenia causes the most grey matter loss.

Why three separate hypotheses?

The NMDA, D1, and GABA-A maps are correlated across the cortex (r ≈ 0.5–0.75) — regions rich in one receptor tend to be rich in others. This is important to know because it means the three conditions aren't perfectly independent tests.

But despite this overlap, the spatial patterns differ enough to produce distinct simulated FC fingerprints — which is why you can still compare conditions and ask which best explains SCZ.

04 — Your Project
Putting it all together:
the schizophrenia question

Your model combines all three papers into a single pipeline. The research question is: which receptor system — NMDA, D1, or GABA-A — when perturbed according to its real spatial density map, produces simulated functional connectivity most similar to empirical schizophrenia FC?

This is a mechanistic test of three competing theories of schizophrenia, grounded in real neuroimaging data and real biophysics.

⚡

NMDA Hypofunction

Reduce recurrent excitation (w+) in proportion to NMDA density. Mimics ketamine-like glutamate blockade. Predicts: reduced long-range FC, especially in prefrontal regions.

w+ ↓ by 10% weighted by NMDA map
🔴

D1 Dysregulation

Reduce transfer function gain (a, b) in proportion to D1 density. Mimics reduced dopaminergic modulation of prefrontal excitability. Predicts: blunted prefrontal responses.

a, b ↓ by 10% weighted by D1 map
🔵

GABA-A Deficit

Reduce inhibitory strength (J_I) in proportion to GABA-A density. Mimics parvalbumin interneuron loss. Predicts: runaway local excitation, disrupted inhibitory control.

J_I ↓ by 10% weighted by GABA map
🎲

Null Model

Same perturbation magnitude as NMDA, but spatially scrambled — applied to random regions. Tests whether the spatial pattern matters, not just the perturbation size.

w+ ↓ by 10% at random nodes
What a result looks like

After simulating, you compare each condition's FC against empirical SCZ FC using Spearman r. The winner (highest delta_r = r(sim,SCZ) − r(sim,HC)) is the most likely mechanism.

05 — Reference
Glossary of key terms
Structural Connectivity (SC)
A matrix showing white-matter fibre connections between brain regions. Measured with diffusion MRI. It's the brain's physical wiring — like a road map between cities.
Functional Connectivity (FC)
How correlated the activity is between brain regions during rest. Measured with fMRI BOLD signal. Two regions with high FC are "talking" a lot — even without a direct physical connection.
Synaptic gating variable S
In the Wong-Wang model, S_i represents the fraction of NMDA channels open in region i. It's the single number that tracks each region's "excitation level". Ranges from 0 (silent) to 1 (fully saturated).
Transfer function H(x)
Converts total input current x into a firing rate. Shaped like an S-curve (sigmoid). Parameters a and b set the gain and threshold. D1 dopamine receptors modulate a and b in your model.
Global coupling G
A single multiplier that scales how strongly all SC connections drive the network. G=0: regions are isolated. G too large: entire brain synchronises. The "working point" is the G where simulated FC best matches real FC.
Working point
The critical dynamical regime — near the edge of a bifurcation — where noise-driven fluctuations are just large enough to explore the SC-defined landscape. This is where models best reproduce empirical FC.
PET (Positron Emission Tomography)
An imaging technique that uses radioactive tracers to measure receptor density in vivo. Each tracer binds to a specific receptor type. The resulting brain map shows where that receptor is most concentrated.
NMDA receptor
N-Methyl-D-Aspartate receptor. A glutamate-gated ion channel critical for synaptic plasticity and long-range cortical communication. Ketamine and PCP block NMDA receptors, producing psychosis-like states — hence the NMDA hypofunction hypothesis of schizophrenia.
FIC (Feedback Inhibitory Control)
A mathematical trick where each region's inhibitory strength J_I is adjusted to keep baseline firing at a fixed target. It makes the model easier to tune but requires iterative fitting. Your project disables FIC (J_I = 1.0 fixed) for simplicity.
Euler-Maruyama
The numerical integration method your code uses to simulate the stochastic differential equations. It's the simplest method for systems with noise — update state by a small step dt, add a noise term proportional to √dt. Fast but requires small dt (0.1ms in your code).
Consensus SC
A group-level structural connectivity matrix that only keeps edges present in ≥50% of subjects. Necessary because averaging 27 individual SC matrices fills in "ghost" connections — edges not present in any single subject but appearing due to inter-subject variation.
Spearman r
A correlation measure that compares two sets of numbers by their rank order rather than raw values. Used to compare simulated FC to empirical FC because it's robust to outliers and doesn't assume linearity. Ranges −1 to +1.
